About

I'm Daniel.
I manage Airbnb properties in West Norfolk.

I'm based in Dersingham and I've been hosting in West and North Norfolk long enough to know what actually works here. The PE31 coast is a particular market — short summer peaks, quiet winter months, a specific type of guest, and a property owner profile that's often a second-home owner looking to cover costs rather than build a portfolio.

I became an Airbnb Superhost because I take the standard seriously. The badge requires a 4.8+ rating, a 90%+ response rate, under 1% cancellations, and consistent booking volume measured on Airbnb alone. It's not difficult to achieve — but it does require actually caring about every stay. Every property I manage is held to that standard.

I also have a co-host profile on Airbnb, which means the properties I manage are run under a professional co-host arrangement — not just informal help. Guests see a responsive, verified host. Owners see consistent standards.

"The owners who win in short-term rental are not chasing maximum income. They're buying flexibility, peace of mind, and professional asset management."

That's the approach here. I'm not selling the highest possible income number — I'm selling a property that works without becoming your second job. The income follows from doing the thing properly.

Why Airbnb only

Not a hedge. A decision.

The big management companies list on five platforms simultaneously. That's their hedge against any one platform underperforming.

This is the deliberate opposite.

Every booking platform has its own search algorithm. Those algorithms reward the same signals: review volume, booking frequency, response rate, conversion rate. When you split bookings across Airbnb, Booking.com, and VRBO, you're not amplifying those signals — you're diluting them.

You end up looking like a new listing on three platforms simultaneously, rather than an established, trusted host on one. The compounding effect runs in reverse. Each review on a single platform makes the next booking more likely. Split that across four channels and you never get to compounding — you're always starting over.

The Superhost standard reinforces this. It's measured on Airbnb alone. The moment you're managing stays across multiple OTAs, you're making that standard harder to maintain — and Superhost is the single biggest organic ranking driver on the platform.

Local knowledge

The PE31 market

I understand this area because I live here. Not a franchise sending someone over from Norwich.

🌊

Summer coast traffic

Hunstanton, Heacham, and the coast drive strong summer demand. Minimum stays and seasonal pricing have to be calibrated for this — not applied generically.

🏰

Sandringham proximity

The Sandringham estate creates a specific pull for certain guests — and certain expectations around property standard. The audience is different from the coastal market.

🧀

Burnham Market premium

Properties in the Burnham Market area command a meaningful premium. That premium has to be reflected in pricing and listing presentation — it doesn't happen automatically.

❄️

Quiet winter months

PE31 winters are quiet. Gap-fill strategy, long-stay pricing, and remote-worker demand become more relevant from October. Property owners should plan for this.

💷

Second-home council tax

Many second-home owners in Norfolk now face a double council tax rate. A properly run Airbnb listing can qualify the property for business rates relief instead — changing the financial picture significantly.
